1) Lane Detection

Implementation

For the lane detection task from a video, the steps are as follows:

Gradient Threshold

In the gradient binary image, we compute the gradient in both the x and y directions. We combine these gradients using a linear combination. Edges are extracted if the gradient falls within the range defined by the minimum threshold (50) and maximum threshold (150).

Color Threshold

We utilize the HSL color space for this task. In scenarios other than snow, we binarize images using the Saturation (S) channel. Road lines, being white and yellow, typically exhibit higher saturation compared to road surfaces. This approach is more robust in various lighting conditions. However, in snowy conditions, we switch to the L color space for binarization, leveraging the significant contrast between the road surface and snow.

Perspective Transform

The perspective transform involves selecting coordinates representing the ends of the left and right lanes as the source points. The destination points correspond to the corners of the original images ([0, 0], [w, 0], [0, h], [w, h]). To ensure robust lane detection across different car orientations, we account for the window frame when the car is turning, guaranteeing accurate detection of left and right lanes.

Lane Fitting

The data obtained from the perspective transform is utilized to fit a second-order polynomial line.

2) Lateral Controller

Implementation

We integrate a lateral controller into a vehicle, which uses the Pure Pursuit algorithm to calculate the steering angle. This algorithm focuses on a look-ahead point positioned at a set distance ahead on the vehicle's path. Our method involves setting the average of the two upcoming unreached waypoints as the look-ahead point. By doing this, the vehicle can anticipate its path better, leading to smoother turns and maintaining its course towards the next target point. This approach not only improves driving stability but also simplifies the model.

3) Particle Filters

Implementation

Lidar readings from 8 directions around the vehicle are utilized for vehicle localization using particle filtering. The process involves sampling particle states, calculating weights, and resampling particles to refine the vehicle's position estimate.
